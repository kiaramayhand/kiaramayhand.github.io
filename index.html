RWE Data Analysis: Dissertation

Risks of Major Adverse Kidney Events

Using Cox proportional hazards models on over 1 million Black and White adults with diabetes or hypertension from the CURE-CKD Registry, this project examined how race, social context, and clinical factors influence risk of major adverse kidney events (MAKE). Black patients were younger yet faced significantly higher adjusted MAKE risk than White patients, with non-commercial insurance, higher social vulnerability index, frequent hospitalizations, and urban residence emerging as top predictors. Findings suggest that disparities in MAKE risk are driven by neighborhood social and structural conditions rather than demographic and clinical characteristics alone.

Machine Learning

Do Machine Learning Models Perform Better than Traditional Cox Proportional Hazard Models?

This project compared the predictive performance of Cox proportional hazards models against machine learning approaches — logistic regression, random forest, and XGBoost — for estimating risk of major adverse kidney events (MAKE) in Black and White patients with diabetes and hypertension using CURE-CKD registry data. XGBoost achieved the highest predictive performance, though differences were modest, suggesting ML and traditional survival analyses are best viewed as complementary tools. SHAP values identified hospitalizations, insurance type, age, baseline eGFR, and social vulnerability index as the most influential predictors.

RWE Data Analysis

Clinical Characteristics and CKD Care Delivery

Using EHR data from over 381,000 patients in the CURE-CKD Registry, this project examined racial disparities in guideline-directed CKD care among Black and American Indian/Alaska Native patients compared to White peers. While Black and AI/AN patients showed modestly higher odds of receiving key medications and diagnostic testing, overall rates were suboptimal across all groups. Findings suggest the disproportionate burden of CKD in these communities cannot be attributed to care delivery alone.

Mixed-Methods Research

Race-Neutral eGFR: Policy, Provider & Patient Perspectives

This mixed-methods project offers a multi-level analysis of de-implementing the race-based eGFR modifier — which historically inflated kidney function estimates for Black patients, delaying CKD diagnosis and access to specialty care. Drawing on semi-structured interviews with national policy leaders, healthcare providers, and patients across two safety-net hospital systems, key themes include challenges in communicating the change, the need for system-wide multidisciplinary coordination, and the importance of pairing algorithmic reform with broader structural investments in equitable care. The study is guided by Public Health Critical Race Praxis and the RE-AIM implementation science framework.

Population Health Research

Colorectal Cancer Screening Adherence

This analysis of 526 multiethnic adults investigated how neighborhood and individual socioeconomic factors, along with race/ethnicity, contribute to colorectal cancer screening non-adherence. Using LASSO and mixed-effect logistic regression on population health survey data, key barriers identified included home rental, perceived discrimination, low healthcare quality, and neighborhood deprivation. Findings highlight that socioeconomic conditions at both the individual and community level are meaningful drivers of the ~25% non-adherence rate among US adults.
